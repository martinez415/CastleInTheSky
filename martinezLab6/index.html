Castle in the Sky

Movie Description

“Castle in the Sky” (also known as "Laputa: Castle in the Sky") is a 1986 animated film directed by Hayao Miyazaki and produced by Studio Ghibli. The film follows a young girl named Sheeta and a boy named Pazu as they embark on a quest to discover the secrets of the legendary floating city of Laputa, while being pursued by a group of military agents and pirates who seek to find the city for themselves.

The film is set in a steampunk-inspired world, with fantastical flying machines and technological wonders mixed with elements of nature and magic. Miyazaki's signature themes of environmentalism, pacifism, and the power of imagination are also present in the film.

"Castle in the Sky" was a critical and commercial success upon its release and has since become a beloved classic in the anime and animation communities. Its stunning animation, thrilling adventure, and heartfelt characters continue to captivate audiences around the world.

Film Development

At the time of production, the animated film had a budget of ¥500 million (or $3,000,000). This matched the record set by an earlier Miyazki film “The Castle of Cagliostro” released in 1979. The film was released in Japan on August 2, 1986. At the box office, it grossed ¥1.6 billion ($8,100,000). The film has multiple release and re-releases in countries such as China, France, the United Kingdom and the United States and accumulated combined worldwide box office gross of around $16,065,703

Prior to the development of “Castle in the Sky”, Miyazaki created the anime series “Future Boy Conan”, which served as inspiration and featured elements which he adapted for Laputa. Characters served as references and plots had similarities to the film. The name “Luputa” is derived from the novel “Gullivers Travels" by Jonathan Swift. In the novel, Laputa is “flying island propelled by a giant central crystal and controlled by its citizens.” Critics reference the novel to the film due to their similarities, citing both explore concepts of technology, political control, and magical realism.

Additionally, Miyazaki took inspiration from Welsh architecture. This can be seen in the beginning of the film where we are first introduced to Pazu as he is working and notices Sheeta floating down into the mine he is working in. While visiting the country in 1984, the director visited mining towns and witnessed a labor strike by local miners. Reflecting on this experience, Miyazaki said: “I was in Wales just after the miners' strike. I really admired the way the miners' unions fought to the very end for their jobs and communities, and I wanted to reflect the strength of those communities in my film." He continues that the miners reminded him a similar events which took place in his native Japan.
